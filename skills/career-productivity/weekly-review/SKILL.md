# Weekly Review

## Overview

A task system is trustworthy only if it's provably complete — the weekly sweep to zero is what lets an anxious brain stop double-checking. The review's other halves: triage against reality (the six-week-old item is dead or avoided — decide which), a plan that's commitments-with-calendar-time rather than a wishlist, and the retro ratio that calibrates next week's honesty.

## When to Use

- Setting up a personal weekly review, or reviving one that lapsed.
- Symptoms: tasks slipping, a todo list nobody trusts, Mondays spent reconstructing state.

**When NOT to use:**
- Team status — `stakeholder-update` (this review drafts it as a byproduct).
- Daily planning — downstream of this.

## Prerequisites

- Some capture system — any trusted place tasks land. Without one, pick anything and use it for a week first.

## The Workflow

1. **Protect the slot: 30–60 minutes, same time weekly, a meeting with yourself.** Friday afternoon or Sunday evening both work; "when I have time" doesn't — skip-rate is the practice's health metric. Hard weeks get the 15-minute degraded version (steps 2+5 only); skips compound into the graveyard state that makes restarting expensive.

2. **Sweep every capture point to zero:** inboxes (email, saved messages, browser tabs, notes, meeting notes — `meeting-notes-actions`' output lands here, desk, head — the 2-minute brain dump routinely surfaces the week's most important loose end). Everything becomes: a next action (concrete verb + object — "email Sam the draft", not "website stuff"), a waiting-for entry (WITH who and since-when), calendar, someday/maybe, reference, or trash. Completeness is what makes the system trustable.

3. **Triage the existing lists against reality:** per active project — what's the actual next action, still true? The item copied forward six weeks is asking a question: dead (delete, with relief) or avoided (usually: the next action is undefined or scary — split it smaller). The waiting-for list gets its chase pass: everything aging past expectation gets a nudge scheduled — most "I dropped it" incidents are un-tracked waiting-fors.

4. **Review the horizon above the tasks, briefly:** calendar 2–3 weeks out (the 10-minute prep task scheduled NOW beats the panic version); active projects vs actual priorities (`prioritization-frameworks`' forcing question, personal edition: "if only three advance this month, which?"); someday/maybe skimmed in 30 seconds.

5. **Plan the coming week as commitments, not a wishlist:** 3–5 outcomes that would make the week a win, the big ones given calendar time (`deep-work-scheduling` owns the block mechanics — a priority without scheduled time is a hope), total load sanity-checked against actual available hours with slack for the guaranteed surprises.

6. **Close with the 5-minute retro:** done-vs-planned ratio (chronically 50% → halve the commitments, not double the guilt — the ratio is calibration data); what interrupted (recurring interruptions are systemic fixes waiting); ONE process tweak max (the review that redesigns itself weekly never stabilizes).

7. **Feed the outward artifacts from the exhaust:** the `stakeholder-update` draft (progress/risks/asks now takes 10 minutes — the review already assembled it), the 1:1 agenda, the `promotion-packet` evidence file (the week's wins captured NOW, while the numbers are findable — review-season you will not remember July). The byproducts are half the ROI.

## Common Rationalizations

| Excuse | Why it doesn't hold |
|---|---|
| "I'll skip the sweep and just plan — that's the useful part" | Planning atop an untrusted system plans fiction, and the graveyard grows underneath. The sweep IS what makes the plan mean anything. |
| "Two missed reviews — the system failed, back to improvising" | The all-or-nothing collapse is the practice's main killer. The 15-minute degraded version exists precisely for hard weeks; consistency beats completeness. |
| "'Handle the migration' is clear enough as a task" | It's sat untouched a month because un-actionable phrasing IS the procrastination mechanism. Verb + object ('email Sam the cutover date') is the fix, not more willpower. |
| "I delegated it — it's off my list" | Untracked waiting-fors are where dropped balls actually live (the example's 9-day blocked colleague). Delegated means ON the list, with who and since-when. |
| "Forty items planned — ambitious week" | Forty items with zero calendar contact is Friday disappointment pre-ordered. Commitments with scheduled time, count calibrated by last week's ratio. |
| "Old items might matter — keep them all" | The 200-item graveyard is why the system isn't trusted. Six-weeks-untouched items are dead or avoided; deciding which (delete or split) is the triage's whole job. |

## Red Flags

- Capture scattered across seven surfaces, none swept.
- Tasks phrased as topics, aging in place.
- No waiting-for list anywhere.
- Weekly plans with no calendar contact.
- Done-vs-planned ratio unknown; guilt as the only feedback.
- The review skipped exactly on the overwhelming weeks (when it pays most).

## Verification

- [ ] Recurring slot on the calendar; skip-rate tracked (degraded version counts as done).
- [ ] All capture points enumerated and swept to zero this week — list of points written.
- [ ] Every active project has a true, verb-object next action.
- [ ] Waiting-for list current with ages; overdue items nudged.
- [ ] Week plan: 3–5 commitments, big ones calendar-blocked, load ≤ available hours.
- [ ] Retro done: ratio recorded, one tweak max; byproducts (update draft, evidence file) fed.

## Example

Engineer, symptoms textbook: 200-item graveyard, two dropped commitments in a month (both un-tracked waiting-fors), Sundays anxious. Setup: Friday 4pm slot, capture consolidated to two points. First review: 70 minutes, painful — the head-dump surfaced 23 loose ends; the graveyard triaged with 60% deleted (with relief), 15 redefined into real next actions. Week 2's chase pass caught the code review a colleague had been silently blocked on for 9 days — the exact class of previously-dropped ball. By week 6: review at 35 minutes, done-vs-planned stabilized ~80% after the retro halved commitment counts, and the recurring-interruption finding (ad-hoc questions, 4/day) became office-hours blocks. Byproducts made it stick: the Friday update now writes itself, and at review season the promotion evidence file had 20 dated, quantified entries where the previous year had memory. The practice survived a brutal month via three consecutive 15-minute degraded versions — and that survival, not any single review, is what kept the system trusted.
