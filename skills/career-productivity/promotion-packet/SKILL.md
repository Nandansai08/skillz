# Promotion Packet

## When to use this skill
- A promotion cycle approaches (ideally: months before), or the year-round evidence habit needs installing.
- Writing the self-review/promo doc, or advising someone through theirs.
- NOT for the comp conversation that follows (`salary-negotiation` step 6) or external applications (`resume-tailoring` — same evidence, different framing).

## Prerequisites
- The next level's actual rubric/ladder document — promotion cases argue against a specific written standard; without it you're arguing against a guess (no published ladder? Your manager's articulation of the bar, in writing, is the substitute to extract FIRST).

## Workflow

1. **Run the evidence file year-round — the step that decides everything downstream:** a running document where wins land WHEN THEY HAPPEN, each entry: date, what, impact with numbers, link (PR, doc, dashboard, the thank-you message), and which rubric dimension it feeds. The `weekly-review` step 7 feed makes this a 2-minute weekly habit. The alternative — reconstructing a year from memory in packet week — loses the numbers (the dashboard that's since changed, the metric nobody screenshotted) and the quieter half of the wins entirely; packet quality is mostly determined 6+ months before packet season.

2. **Gap-analyze against the rubric before writing anything:** per dimension of the next level — strong evidence / thin evidence / genuine gap. This is the conversation to have with your manager EARLY ("what would make the technical-leadership dimension undeniable?") because gaps identified two quarters out are fillable (volunteer for the cross-team design, take the incident-command rotation); gaps discovered at packet time are next cycle's news. The manager-alignment itself is half the game: a promotion your manager is surprised by is a promotion that isn't happening.

3. **Frame every claim as impact, not activity:** the ladder's actual question is "what changed because you operated at the next level?" — so each packet claim runs: business/team outcome + your specific role + evidence link. "Redesigned the deploy pipeline" (activity) becomes "Cut deploy time 45→8 min (link), unblocking the daily-release goal — I identified the bottleneck, designed the fix, and led two engineers through the migration" (impact + role + scale). The `resume-tailoring` step 3 conversion, held to an in-house standard of proof: reviewers here CAN check the links.

4. **Calibrate scope claims to the level's altitude:** levels are distinguished by scope and autonomy, not effort — the packet shows next-level scope ALREADY OPERATING ("influenced across three teams", "set the direction others executed", "identified the problem nobody had named"), because most ladders promote on demonstrated-at-level, not potential. Claim your true role precisely: "led" that was "participated" gets found by the calibration committee's cross-checks (someone in that room was there), and one inflated claim discounts the packet's honest majority — while the inverse failure, systematically claiming solo wins as "we", makes a committee conclude the team did it all (the modesty tax is real, especially for those trained to pay it: state your role in the first person, accurately).

5. **Weave the claims into a narrative, not a list:** the strongest packets read as a story — "over this period I moved from [prior scope] to [next-level scope]: [arc of 3–4 flagship examples, each rubric-mapped]" — because committees read dozens of packets and remember arcs, not bullet piles. Flagships get depth (the full impact-role-evidence treatment); the supporting cast gets a line each. Address the known weak dimension honestly with its counter-evidence rather than hoping it goes unnoticed (it won't; the pre-emption reads as self-awareness, the omission as blindness).

6. **Recruit the supporting voices deliberately:** peer/stakeholder feedback (where the process includes it) briefed with specifics — "would you speak to the migration work we did?" gets a paragraph of generic praise; "the committee will probe cross-team influence — the API-negotiation with your team is my best evidence, could you speak to that specifically?" gets testimony that lands. Give your endorsers the packet's claims relevant to them; they can't corroborate what they don't know you're claiming.

7. **If the answer is no, convert it into a contract:** the debrief question is "which specific dimension, and what evidence would change the answer, by when?" — written down (`salary-negotiation` step 6's same move), turned into next cycle's step-2 gap plan with your manager co-owning it. A vague no repeated twice is also information (`technology-evaluation`'s exit-pricing instinct, applied to your own trajectory — sometimes the fastest promotion is at a different company, where `resume-tailoring` inherits this packet's evidence file wholesale).

## Common pitfalls
- Packet-week archaeology: the year reconstructed from memory — numberless claims, forgotten wins, and the packet reads exactly as thin as its evidence trail (step 1 is the fix, installed twelve months too early or not at all).
- Activity lists: twenty things done, zero things changed — the committee's question is impact, and effort-volume actively signals current-level operation (step 3).
- The surprise packet: manager learns of the ambition at packet time — no gap-filling happened, no advocacy was built, and the packet argues alone (step 2's early alignment).
- Inflation and its mirror: the "led" that wasn't (found, fatal) and the "we" that erases you (unfound, equally fatal) — precision in both directions (step 4).
- Rubric-free writing: a beautiful narrative that maps to no dimension of the actual ladder — committees score against the rubric; unmapped excellence scores as noise (steps 2, 5).
- Treating a no as a verdict instead of a data point: no debrief, no contract, same packet next cycle — or the vague-no pattern unexamined for what it's actually saying (step 7).

## Example
Senior engineer targeting staff, six months out. Rubric gap-analysis with manager: technical execution strong, cross-team influence THIN, technical direction genuine gap. The two quarters bought by asking early: volunteered to lead the cross-team API-versioning effort (filling influence) and wrote the team's first architecture-direction doc for the queue migration (filling direction) — both landing in the evidence file with dates, links, and rubric tags alongside 30 weekly-review-fed entries. Packet written in packet week from the file in two evenings (versus the peer whose reconstruction took two weekends and still lacked numbers): narrative arc "execution → org-level leverage," three flagships — the versioning effort (impact: unblocked two teams' roadmaps, link to the adopted RFC; role: "I drove the negotiation between three teams' conflicting constraints" — first person, precise), the migration direction doc (adopted, linked), and an incident-command example addressing the known weak spot (operational leadership) head-on. Endorsers briefed with specific claims; the partner-team lead's feedback spoke directly to the negotiation. Committee outcome: promoted; the debrief noted the influence evidence as decisive — the dimension that had been THIN six months earlier, fillable only because it was found early. The evidence file's next entry: the promotion itself, dated, for the eventual `resume-tailoring` run.
