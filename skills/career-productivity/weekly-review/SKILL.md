# Weekly Review

## When to use this skill
- Setting up a personal weekly review, or reviving one that lapsed.
- Symptoms present: tasks slipping through cracks, a todo list nobody trusts, Monday mornings spent reconstructing state.
- NOT for team-level status (`stakeholder-update`) or daily planning (the review FEEDS daily planning; it isn't it).

## Prerequisites
- Some capture system — any trusted place tasks/commitments land (app, file, notebook). The review maintains a system; without one, start by picking anything and using it for a week.

## Workflow

1. **Protect the slot: 30–60 minutes, same time weekly, treated as a meeting with yourself.** Friday afternoon (closes the week, Monday starts oriented) or Sunday evening both work; what doesn't work is "when I have time" — the review IS the practice, and its skip-rate is the practice's health metric. Shortcut for hard weeks: a 15-minute degraded version (steps 2+5 only) beats a skip; skips compound into the graveyard state that makes restarting expensive.

2. **Sweep every capture point to zero:** inboxes (email, Slack saved-items, browser tabs, notes app, meeting notes — `meeting-notes-actions`' output lands here, desk pile, head — the 2-minute brain dump of "what's nagging me" routinely surfaces the week's most important loose end). Everything becomes: a next action (concrete verb + object — "email Sam the draft" not "website stuff"), a waiting-for entry (delegated/blocked items WITH who and since-when), calendar (time-bound), someday/maybe, reference, or trash. The sweep's completeness is what makes the system trustable — a system that might be missing things gets double-checked by an anxious brain, which is the tax the review eliminates.

3. **Triage the existing lists against reality:** per active project — what's the actual next action, and is it still true? Stale tasks get renewed, redefined, or deliberately dropped (the item copied forward six weeks is asking a question: dead ("delete, with relief") or avoided (usually: the next action is undefined or scary — split it smaller). The waiting-for list gets its chase pass: everything aging past its expectation gets a nudge scheduled (this list is where dropped balls actually live — most "I dropped it" incidents are un-tracked waiting-fors).

4. **Review the horizon above the tasks, briefly:** calendar 2–3 weeks out (prep needed for anything looming? travel, reviews, deadlines — the 10-minute prep task scheduled NOW beats the panic version), active-projects list vs actual priorities (a project list that's all motion and no priority-alignment is a busy-trap — `prioritization-frameworks`' forcing question, personal edition: "if only three advance this month, which?"), and the someday/maybe skim (30 seconds; promote or leave).

5. **Plan the coming week as commitments, not a wishlist:** pick the 3–5 outcomes that would make the week a win (tied to step 4's priorities), give the big ones calendar time (`deep-work-scheduling` owns the block mechanics — a priority without scheduled time is a hope), and sanity-check total load against actual available hours (the 40-item weekly plan is a Friday disappointment pre-ordered; the honest version has slack for the week's guaranteed surprises).

6. **Close with the 5-minute retro:** what got done vs planned (the ratio trend is your planning calibration — chronically at 50% means halve the commitments, not double the guilt), what interrupted (recurring interruption patterns are systemic fixes waiting — the meeting that should be an email, the alert that should be a ticket: `alerting-design` step 2's actionability question, applied to your own week), and one process tweak max (the review that redesigns itself weekly never stabilizes).

7. **Feed the outward-facing artifacts from the review's exhaust:** the `stakeholder-update` draft (the week's progress/risks/asks now takes 10 minutes — the review already assembled it), the 1:1 agenda, the `promotion-packet` evidence file (the week's wins captured NOW, while the numbers are findable — future-you at review season will not remember July). The review's byproducts are half its ROI.

## Common pitfalls
- The review that only plans: skipping the sweep and triage (steps 2–3) for the fun part (step 5) — planning atop an untrusted system plans fiction; the graveyard grows underneath.
- Capture-point sprawl: seven inboxes, three apps, sticky notes — the sweep can't reach zero across unbounded surfaces. Consolidate ruthlessly; every capture point is a weekly tax.
- Vague next actions: "handle the migration" sitting untouched for a month — un-actionable phrasing IS the procrastination mechanism; the verb-object rewrite (step 2) is the fix, not more willpower.
- Waiting-for amnesia: delegated items tracked nowhere, resurfacing as fires — the list (step 3) plus the chase pass is the whole defense.
- Plan-as-wishlist: 40 items, zero calendar contact, guilt by Friday — commitments with scheduled time (step 5), count calibrated by the retro's ratio (step 6).
- All-or-nothing collapse: two missed reviews read as failure, practice abandoned — the 15-minute degraded version (step 1) is the practice's survival mode; consistency beats completeness.

## Example
Engineer, symptoms textbook: 200-item task graveyard, two dropped commitments in a month (both un-tracked waiting-fors), Sundays anxious. Setup: Friday 4pm slot, capture consolidated to two points (one app + email). First review: 70 minutes, painful — the sweep surfaced 23 loose ends from the head-dump alone; the graveyard triaged with 60% deleted ("with relief" — most were dead), 15 redefined into real next actions. Week 2's chase pass caught the code-review a colleague had been silently blocked on for 9 days — the exact class of previously-dropped ball. By week 6: review at 35 minutes, done-vs-planned ratio stabilized at ~80% after the retro halved initial commitment counts (calibration working as designed), and the recurring-interruption finding (ad-hoc "quick questions" averaging 4/day) became office-hours blocks — the systemic fix the retro exists to surface. Byproduct value made the habit stick: the Friday `stakeholder-update` now writes itself from the review, and at review season the promotion evidence file had 20 dated, quantified entries where the previous year had memory. The practice survived a brutal month via three consecutive 15-minute degraded versions — and that survival, not any single review, is what kept the system trusted.
