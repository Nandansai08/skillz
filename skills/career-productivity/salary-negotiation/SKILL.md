# Salary Negotiation

## Overview

The no-ask outcome compounds through every future raise and next-job anchor — and a professional ask has never rescinded a real offer. Three numbers written before any conversation, the full package evaluated before any response, one consolidated ask, and the level lever recognized as the one that outvalues base for years.

## When to Use

- An offer is in hand or imminent, or a raise conversation is being planned.
- The "what are your comp expectations?" question is coming and needs a plan.

**When NOT to use:**
- Legal review of unusual equity documents — real counsel.

## Prerequisites

- Market data gathered BEFORE any numbers conversation: levels.fyi / recruiter-reported bands for role, level, location — plus the company's actual band if discoverable. Negotiating without data is asking the counterparty to grade your guess.

## The Workflow

1. **Establish your three numbers privately, from data:** target (justified by the market data's upper half for your evidence), acceptable floor (below which you walk — decided NOW, calmly), and your real BATNA (current job, other processes, honestly assessed — negotiating strength is mostly this number). Write them down; deadline pressure erodes unwritten resolve.

2. **Deflect premature number-naming, then anchor high when naming becomes right:** early "expected comp?" gets "I'd rather understand the role's scope first — what's the band for this level?" (many jurisdictions now require an answer). If forced to name first: anchor at target-or-above with justification, never a range whose bottom you'd resent (ranges get heard as their minimum). Recovering from a low self-anchor is nearly impossible; gracious movement from a high one is easy.

3. **Evaluate the FULL package before responding:** base, bonus (target vs guaranteed, payout history), equity (private: strike, valuation, preferences, exercise window — paper discounted accordingly; public: vest schedule), sign-on (the easiest lever — one-time money moves freest), plus the non-cash levers: **level itself (the biggest one — a level negotiation outvalues a base negotiation for years)**, remote terms, start date, review-timing acceleration ("first review at 6 months" ≈ a raise pull-forward). Convert everything to a comparable annual number before comparing.

4. **Negotiate with evidence and enthusiasm, in ONE consolidated ask:** genuine excitement + specific ask + justification ("I'm excited about this team. Based on [market data / competing offer], I was expecting base closer to X and would sign at [package]. Can we get there?"). Consolidate the levers that matter — the sequential drip (base agreed, then "also sign-on," then "also start date") spends goodwill per round; the fifth "one more thing" sours a yes. Competing offers stated factually, never bluffed — called bluffs end leverage AND goodwill.

5. **Navigate the standard hard moments with prepared lines:**
   - "That's above the band" → "Is the band for this level? Given [evidence], would the next level's band fit the scope we discussed?" (redirects to the level lever).
   - Exploding deadline → "I want to decide well, not fast — can we extend to [date]?" (usually yes; genuine ones are rarer than claimed).
   - "We need your current salary" → decline where legal: "I'd rather focus on this role's value — my expectation is X."
   - Verbal-only improvements → "That's great — can you send the updated letter?" Everything real is written.

6. **For raises (the internal variant), run a campaign, not a conversation:** evidence assembled `promotion-packet`-style, the ask made BEFORE budget cycles lock (ask your manager when comp planning happens), framed retention-positive, with the follow-up contract if "not now" ("what specifically gets me there, by when?" — converting a soft no into a commitment). A competing offer forces the issue but spends the relationship — use it only when you'd genuinely take the alternative.

7. **Close cleanly and calibrate:** final package in writing before resigning anything; decline paths kept warm (the recruiter refused kindly is next cycle's warm intro); the retro note — what was asked, what moved, what you'd anchor differently — because comp negotiation recurs every 2–4 years and the notes compound.

## Common Rationalizations

| Excuse | Why it doesn't hold |
|---|---|
| "The offer is fair — negotiating seems greedy" | Companies budget for the ask and expect it; the no-ask discount compounds through every future percentage raise. The majority who don't negotiate fund the minority who do. |
| "They might pull the offer if I push" | A professional, enthusiastic, evidence-based ask has never rescinded a real offer — rescission stories trace to ultimatums and bluffs, which this method excludes. |
| "I'll just answer their salary question honestly — transparency" | Naming first, low, before research, is the self-anchor no later skill recovers. Deflection is professional and increasingly legally protected; transparency about your VALUE isn't transparency about your history. |
| "Base salary is the real number — the rest is noise" | Level, sign-on, and review timing moved more money than base in the example — level especially, for YEARS. Base-tunnel-vision negotiates the smallest lever hardest. |
| "I'll mention the other offer even though it's not real" | Bluffs get called ('great, congratulations — we understand'), and the called bluff ends both leverage and trust. Real alternatives stated factually; imaginary ones stated never. |
| "I'll ask for the raise when I'm frustrated enough" | Frustration timing is usually post-budget-lock, evidence-free, and reads as flight risk rather than retention case. The campaign (step 6) asks before the money's allocated, with the packet. |

## Red Flags

- Numbers named before market research existed.
- The floor decided mid-negotiation, under deadline pressure.
- Multiple sequential asks after agreement.
- Package compared on base alone; equity taken at paper value.
- Verbal improvements not yet in writing, resignation already submitted.
- Raise asks landing after comp cycles close, year after year.

## Verification

- [ ] Three numbers written with market-data sources — before the first comp conversation.
- [ ] Full-package conversion done: every offer as a comparable annual number — sheet attached.
- [ ] The ask consolidated (one round covering the chosen levers) — script drafted.
- [ ] Competing offers real or unmentioned — no bluffs.
- [ ] Final terms in writing before any resignation.
- [ ] Retro note filed (asked/moved/learned) for the next cycle.

## Example

Offer: senior role, base 165k, 10% bonus, 60k equity/4yr, no sign-on. Market data: the role's band midpoint ≈ 180k, and the SCOPE discussed matched the next level up (band ≈ 200k). Three numbers written: target 195k-equivalent, floor 175k-equivalent, BATNA solid. Full-package pass: offer ≈ 196k/yr paper, private-equity discounted → ≈ 184k comparable. Consolidated ask, delivered with genuine enthusiasm: the level question first ("the scope we discussed — three-team platform ownership — reads as L+1; can we evaluate at that level?"), plus sign-on and an accelerated first review as flex levers. Company: level bump declined ("committee timing") BUT base to 180k, 25k sign-on, first review at 6 months in writing — ≈ 199k comparable year one, above target. The other process's offer arrived post-signing, declined warmly; that recruiter sourced the candidate's next role three years later. Retro lesson recorded: the level question moved more money than the base question ever would have — lead with it next time.
