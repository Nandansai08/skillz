# Salary Negotiation

## When to use this skill
- An offer is in hand or imminent, or a raise conversation is being planned.
- The "what are your comp expectations?" question is coming and needs a plan.
- NOT legal/contract review (get real counsel for equity documents in unusual structures) — this is the negotiation itself.

## Prerequisites
- Market data gathered BEFORE any numbers conversation: levels.fyi / Glassdoor / recruiter-reported bands for the role, level, and location — plus the company's actual band if discoverable (posted ranges where law requires, peers, recruiters). Negotiating without data is asking the counterparty to grade your guess.

## Workflow

1. **Establish your three numbers privately, from data:** target (justified by the market data's upper-half for your evidence), acceptable floor (below which you walk — decided NOW, calmly, not at the deadline), and walk-away alternatives (current job, other processes — your real BATNA, honestly assessed: negotiating strength is mostly this number). Write them down; deadline pressure erodes unwritten resolve.

2. **Deflect premature number-naming, then anchor high when naming becomes right:** early "expected comp?" gets the deflection ("I'd rather understand the role's scope first — what's the band for this level?" — many jurisdictions now require an answer); if forced to name first, anchor at target-or-above with justification attached, never a range whose bottom you'd resent (ranges get heard as their minimum). The anchor's power is real and one-directional — recovering from a low self-anchor is nearly impossible; from a high one, gracious movement is easy.

3. **Evaluate the FULL package before responding to any offer:** base, bonus (target vs guaranteed — and its actual payout history if discoverable), equity (for private companies: strike price, latest valuation, preferences, exercise window — paper numbers discounted accordingly; for public: vest schedule and cliff), sign-on (the easiest lever — one-time money moves freer than recurring), plus the non-cash levers: level itself (the biggest one — a level negotiation outvalues a base negotiation for years), remote/location terms, start date, PTO, learning budget, review-timing acceleration ("first review at 6 months" ≈ a raise pull-forward). Convert to a comparable annual number per offer before comparing anything.

4. **Negotiate with evidence and enthusiasm, in one consolidated ask:** the frame that works — genuinely enthusiastic about the role + specific ask + justification ("I'm excited about this team. Based on [market data / competing offer / the scope discussed], I was expecting base closer to X and would sign at [package]. Can we get there?"). Consolidate: ONE ask covering the levers that matter, not a sequential drip (each re-negotiation round spends goodwill; the fifth "one more thing" sours a yes). Competing offers stated factually, never as threats — and never bluffed (bluffs get called, and a called bluff ends the leverage AND the goodwill).

5. **Navigate the standard hard moments with prepared lines:**
   - "That's above the band" → "Is the band for this level? Given [evidence], would the next level's band fit the scope we discussed?" (redirects to the level lever).
   - Exploding deadline → "I want to decide well, not fast — can we extend to [date]?" (usually yes; genuine ones exist but are rarer than claimed).
   - "We need your current salary" → decline where legal (increasingly everywhere): "I'd rather focus on this role's value — my expectation is X."
   - Verbal-only improvements → "That's great — can you send the updated letter?" Everything real is written.

6. **For raises (the internal variant), run it as a campaign, not a conversation:** evidence assembled `promotion-packet`-style (impact with numbers, scope growth, market data for your role), the ask made BEFORE budget cycles lock (ask your manager when comp planning happens — asking after is asking for next year), framed as retention-positive ("I want to keep growing here; here's the case"), with the follow-up date agreed if the answer is "not now" ("what specifically gets me there, by when?" — converting a soft no into a contract). A competing offer forces the issue but spends the relationship — use it when you'd genuinely take the alternative.

7. **Close cleanly and calibrate for next time:** final package in writing before resigning anything; decline paths kept warm (the recruiter you refused kindly is next cycle's warm intro); and the retro note — what was asked, what moved, what you'd anchor differently — because comp negotiation recurs every 2–4 years and the notes compound (`interview-prep-technical` step 7's same repeated-game logic).

## Common pitfalls
- Not negotiating at all: the majority case, and the most expensive — the no-ask outcome compounds through every future raise-on-base and next-job anchor. Companies expect the ask; a professional ask has never rescinded a real offer.
- Naming a number before researching one: the self-anchor at the band's bottom, cheerfully accepted by the recruiter within the hour.
- Base-only tunnel vision: winning $5k of base while leaving level, sign-on, and equity untouched — the levers with 10× the range (step 3's full-package view).
- The sequential drip: base agreed, then "also the sign-on," then "also start date" — each round friendlier than the last is not how the receiving side experiences it (step 4's consolidation).
- Bluffed competing offers: called, collapsed, and remembered.
- Deadline capitulation: the exploding offer accepted at the floor because the written floor didn't exist (step 1) and the extension was never requested (step 5).
- Raise-by-ambush: the demand delivered after budget lock, evidence-free, with a bluffed alternative — the trifecta of the failed internal ask (step 6 inverts all three).

## Example
Offer: senior role, base 165k, 10% bonus, 60k equity/4yr, no sign-on. Market data (levels.fyi + two recruiter datapoints): the role's band midpoint ≈ 180k base, and the SCOPE discussed matched the next level up (band ≈ 200k). Three numbers written: target 195k-equivalent, floor 175k-equivalent, BATNA solid (current job fine, one other process at onsite stage). The evaluate-first pass converted everything annual: offer ≈ 196k/yr paper, but private-company equity discounted → ≈ 184k comparable. Consolidated ask, delivered with genuine enthusiasm: the level question first ("the scope we discussed — three-team platform ownership — reads as L+1; can we evaluate at that level?"), plus sign-on and an accelerated first review as the flex levers. Company came back: level bump declined ("committee timing") BUT base to 180k, 25k sign-on, first review at 6 months in writing — ≈ 199k comparable year one, above target. The other process's offer arrived post-signing and was declined warmly; that recruiter sourced the candidate's next role three years later. Retro note's lesson recorded: the level question moved more money than the base question ever would have — lead with it next time.
