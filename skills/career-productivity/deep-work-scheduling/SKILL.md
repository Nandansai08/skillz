# Deep Work Scheduling

## Overview

The audit before the reform: the usual findings are less meeting-load than believed, more fragmentation than believed, and a self-inflicted third of the interruptions nobody blames. Blocks at biological peak, defended structurally, paired with a published open door — because colleagues tolerate bounded unavailability and revolt at ambient unavailability.

## When to Use

- Meaningful work keeps losing to meetings and interrupts; output happens only at night or not at all.
- Designing a personal (or team-level) focus-time system.

**When NOT to use:**
- Choosing the work itself — `weekly-review` step 5 assigns each block's tenant.

## Prerequisites

- One honest week of data: calendar audit + a rough interrupt tally. Reform without the audit fixes the imagined problem.

## The Workflow

1. **Audit the week and name the real enemy:** categorize the calendar (deep work / needed-you meetings / didn't-need-you meetings / reactive time), tally interrupts by source for 2–3 days — including self-inflicted tab-switching, routinely a third of the total. Typical findings: less meeting-load than believed, more fragmentation — 6 "free" hours in 45-minute shards is zero deep-work hours, because sub-90-minute shards rarely reach depth (the fixed cost of loading context dominates).

2. **Place 2–3 blocks of 2–4 hours at your biological peak, recurring:** protect your BEST hours (defending the trough is theater); realistic dose — 2–4 blocks/week held beats an aspirational daily block abandoned by Wednesday; each block has a tenant assigned from the weekly review (an empty-purpose block is the first surrendered).

3. **Make the blocks structurally defensible:** named calendar events (auto-decline where culture permits), notifications actually off (the status not enforced by settings is decoration), phone elsewhere, ONE work surface. The context-switch math justifies the rigor: each "quick check" costs the working-state reload — a 30-second glance with a 15-minute re-immersion tax means four glances convert a deep block into shallow shards.

4. **Pair every closed door with an open one — the interrupt budget:** published availability (office hours, "async always, sync after 2pm") is what makes block-defense socially sustainable. The genuinely-urgent channel stays open and NAMED ("page me via X for prod") — a focus system with no escape hatch gets bypassed at the first real fire, then routinely.

5. **Batch the shallow work into deliberate shards:** email/Slack in 2–3 scheduled passes instead of ambient monitoring; sub-15-minute tasks collected into an admin block; meetings clustered where calendar control permits (two meeting-dense afternoons beat five polka-dotted days — meeting adjacency is nearly free; scatter is what shreds the week).

6. **Enter blocks warm and exit them clean:** a 2-minute entry ritual (yesterday's exit note says exactly where to resume — the "start where?" flail is a 20-minute tax on every cold block) and an exit note when ending mid-thought ("next: the retry test; branch mid-refactor at the pool logic"). The cheapest productivity artifact in this entire skill.

7. **Review monthly with two numbers:** blocks-held rate (chronic surrender = blocks mis-placed, over-dosed, or the open door not yet credible) and longest-unbroken-stretch trend. Team version: shared focus windows (org-wide no-meeting mornings outperform individual heroics — fragmentation is substantially a coordination problem).

## Common Rationalizations

| Excuse | Why it doesn't hold |
|---|---|
| "My calendar is wall-to-wall meetings — nothing to schedule around" | The audit's usual finding: 14 real meeting-hours believed to be 25, with fragmentation as the actual enemy. Measure first; the imagined problem gets the wrong reform. |
| "The block's on my calendar — that's the system" | With notifications on, the event is a label on a shard-generator. Structural defense (settings-enforced silence, one surface) IS the block; the calendar entry is its name. |
| "I'll put focus time at 4pm — mornings fill up" | Surrendering peak hours and defending the trough optimizes the wrong asset. The block placement question is 'when is my focus best,' not 'what's left over.' |
| "Total unavailability — they'll adapt" | They won't; the first genuine fire bypasses the wall, and then everything does. The named urgent channel plus published office hours is what makes the closed door hold. |
| "A quick Slack glance costs 30 seconds" | Plus the 15-minute working-state reload. Four glances convert the deep block into shallow shards — the interruption's cost was never its duration. |
| "Daily 4-hour blocks starting Monday — go big" | Abandoned by Wednesday, system discredited by Friday. The sustainable dose held for months beats the heroic one held for days; the held-rate metric is the honesty check. |

## Red Flags

- Focus blocks scheduled and routinely surrendered.
- "Deep work" events with Slack notifications active.
- No published availability window anywhere.
- Six 45-minute gaps counted as six hours of capacity.
- No entry/exit notes; every block starting with a 20-minute flail.
- The interrupt tally never taken; meetings blamed on faith.

## Verification

- [ ] Week audited: calendar categorized, interrupts tallied by source (incl. self-inflicted) — numbers written.
- [ ] Blocks placed at peak hours, recurring, tenant-assigned — calendar shown.
- [ ] Defense structural: notification settings verified off during blocks — settings checked.
- [ ] Open door published (hours + urgent channel named) — link/message shown.
- [ ] Entry/exit notes in use — last block's exit note exists.
- [ ] Monthly review running: blocks-held rate and longest-stretch trend recorded.

## Example

Senior engineer, complaint: "zero focus time, calendar is meetings." Audit's actual findings: 14 meeting-hours (not the believed 25), but no unbroken stretch over 50 minutes all week — and the interrupt tally: 31/day, of which 12 self-inflicted check-ins. System: two 3-hour morning blocks (Tue/Thu, peak, recurring, auto-decline) + one 90-minute Friday block; tenants from the weekly review. Defense: OS-level focus mode (the Slack status alone had already failed), urgent channel named (pager for prod — and the team told exactly that). Open door: daily 2–4pm office hours — which made the mornings socially survivable; the one colleague who tested the boundary switched to office hours within two weeks. Entry/exit notes adopted after the first cold-start flail. Month-two numbers: blocks-held 10 of 12; longest stretch 3h (from 50min); the two surrendered blocks both traced to a genuine incident — the escape hatch used as designed, credibility intact. The migration that had crawled for a quarter shipped in five weeks of Tuesday-Thursdays — the before/after that converted two teammates, whose adoption birthed the team's shared no-meeting mornings.
