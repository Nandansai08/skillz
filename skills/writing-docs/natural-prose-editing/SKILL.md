# Natural Prose Editing

## When to use this skill
- The text is technically correct but reads flat, generic, or interchangeable
  with anything else on the topic.
- Someone says a draft "sounds like AI" or "doesn't sound like me."
- A blog post, email, or report needs a pass before it goes out.
- NOT for evading AI-detection tools on academic or dishonest submissions —
  this skill is about writing quality, not detector evasion, and should be
  refused if that's the stated goal.

## Prerequisites
- The original draft or source text.
- Ideally, a few samples of the person's own writing (email, past posts) to
  match voice — without it, aim for plain, specific, unhedged prose rather
  than a fabricated "personality."

## Workflow

1. **Read once for content, not style.** Confirm the claims and structure are
   sound before touching wording — don't polish something that's wrong.

2. **Strip cliché AI phrasing.** Flag and rewrite stock openers/closers and
   hedges:
   - "It's important to note that…" → just state the thing.
   - "In today's fast-paced world…" → cut entirely, start with the point.
   - "This isn't just X, it's Y" (false-contrast construction) → say what it
     actually is.
   - "Overall," "In conclusion," "Furthermore" as paragraph openers → delete
     or replace with a real transition tied to content.
   - Excessive hedging ("could potentially," "it's worth considering") →
     commit to a claim or cut it.

3. **Break up rule-of-three / triad addiction.** AI-generated text leans hard
   on three-item lists and three-clause sentences ("clear, concise, and
   compelling"). Vary it: some sentences get one strong word, some get a
   longer list, some get none.

4. **Vary sentence length and rhythm.** Look for runs of same-length,
   same-structure sentences. Break some short. Let one run long. Read it out
   loud — natural writing doesn't march in lockstep.

5. **Replace vague abstractions with concrete specifics.** "This leads to
   better outcomes" → name the outcome. "Many users report issues" → say
   which issue, roughly how many, or cut the unsupported claim.

6. **Cut throat-clearing.** Delete sentences that restate the topic before
   getting to the point ("When it comes to X, there are many factors to
   consider"). Start with the actual first fact or claim.

7. **Match voice, if a sample exists.** Check contractions, sentence length,
   formality, and characteristic phrases against the person's real writing
   samples; adjust rather than imposing a generic "conversational" tone.

8. **Read the final pass end to end.** Confirm nothing was smoothed into
   blandness in the process — natural prose still has an edge or opinion, not
   just fewer clichés.

## Common pitfalls
- Over-correcting into forced quirkiness (random slang, exclamation points)
  to prove it's "not AI" — reads worse than the original.
- Removing all hedges even where genuine uncertainty exists — sometimes
  "might" is the accurate word.
- Fabricating a voice with no reference sample, producing generic
  "conversational" tone that isn't actually the person's.
- Treating this as a find-and-replace of banned words — the underlying
  problem is usually structure and rhythm, not any single phrase.
- Using this skill to help evade AI-content detectors rather than to improve
  genuine writing quality — out of scope, decline.

## Example

**Before:**
> It's important to note that in today's competitive landscape, effective
> communication is not just important, it's essential. Furthermore, clear,
> concise, and compelling writing can help you stand out.

**After:**
> Clear writing gets read. Everything else is competing for an inbox that's
> already full — so cut the sentence that doesn't earn its place.
